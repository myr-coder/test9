Moments of mass book bans through history

While going through the data, I noticed that over the years, there were moments when entire countries imposed mass book bans at the same time. This caught my attention, so I decided to dig deeper and find out why. Apart from the well-known censorship in Nazi Germany, I found several key events before the year 2000 where governments or authorities banned large numbers of books. Hereâ€™s what I discovered.

149 books in 80 years

New Zealand

From the 1910s to the 1990s, book bans in New Zealand increased due to strict censorship laws. The Indecent Publications Act 1910 and the Customs Act 1913 gave authorities the power to search for and seize books they considered "indecent." Censorship remained strict until the 1990s when the Classification Office took over.

34 books in 50 years

India

There was an increase of book bans in India from the 1940s. In the aftermath of Partition, bans on specific books from across the border came into force. Politics, and what the state often saw as the misrepresentation of either India's policies or its leaders, triggered most book bans during this period

34 books in 30 years

China

In the 1980s, Zhao Ziyangâ€™s leadership allowed more freedom in publishing, making information about censorship more available. After the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests, many books were banned. There was a short period of relaxed rules from the mid-1990s to mid-2000s, but in 2012, when Xi Jinping came to power, tighter control over books on liberal ideas returned.

27 books in less than 5 years

Singapore

In 1960, Singapore introduced the Internal Security Act, giving the government power to ban books and other materials it saw as a threat to national security.

14 books in 50 years

Australia

From 1920 to 1970, Australia banned a large number of books under strict Customs laws, allowing officials to search luggage and seize banned titles. Books were censored if they were thought as obscene, blasphemous or seditious. Change began in 1969 when Customs Minister Don Chipp easing restrictions, and by 1972, Prime Minister Gough Whitlamâ€™s government dismantled the system.

Censorship hotspots: A global look

A world map was created showing number of book bans across different countries, with the highest numbers coming from Asia but the highest country being New Zealand. When filtering by government type, the data reveals that most bans come from countries that are currently constitutional monarchies, followed by parliamentary republics. However, it's important to note that due to limited information from places like North Korea, some countries are omitted from this map.

What genres get banned the most?

All the banned books were categorized into 18 genres and cross-referenced with different types of governments. Fiction stood out as the most frequently banned genre, followed by non-fiction. Interestingly, countries that are currently Constitutional monarchies had the highest number of banned fiction books. These governments also led in banning books with erotic or sexual content, often labeling them as "obscene." This suggests that censorship in these nations tends to target not just political themes in fiction but also content that challenges social norms around sexuality

Methodology

Data on book bans from Wikipedia, Google Books and various educational databases.

Data on current government status from The World Factbook.

We started setting up the data by cross-referencing multiple sources since not all book bans are officially documented. Only books that were formally banned, removed from circulation, or restricted by a governing authority were included in this dataset. For books with missing genre data, we used the Google Books API to classify them. The final dataset was visualized using Tableau and along with coding for interactivity.

Itâ€™s important to note that some bans remain undocumentedâ€”especially in authoritarian regimes like North Korea and Nazi Germany, where censorship often occurs without public records or through the controlled distribution of approved literature.

This dataset is only a snapshot of patterns in literary censorship, rather than an exhaustive record of every book banned.
